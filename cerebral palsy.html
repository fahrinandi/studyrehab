Exam principle: CP is a non-progressive brain lesion, but its musculoskeletal, respiratory, nutritional, gastrointestinal and participation consequences can progress. Your answer should distinguish the primary neural problem from secondary impairments and compensations. [U3][W1]

1. Definition and diagnostic frame

Cerebral palsy is a group of permanent disorders of movement and posture development causing activity limitation, attributed to non-progressive disturbances in the developing fetal or infant brain. [U1][R1]

Diagnostic criterion 1

Neuromotor control deficit that alters movement or posture.

Diagnostic criterion 2

Static brain lesion, meaning the original brain injury is non-progressive.

Diagnostic criterion 3

Timing in early development, acquired before birth, during birth, or in the first years of life.

B. By anatomical distribution

Hemiplegia

One side of body affected. Upper limb often more involved than lower limb. Sensory deficits, stereognosis and fine motor limitations are important to test.

Diplegia

Lower limbs more affected than upper limbs. Common in prematurity and periventricular leukomalacia. Gait and orthosis planning are central.

Quadriplegia

All four limbs, trunk, head control and oromotor function may be involved. Often higher comorbidity and higher GMFCS level.

Monoplegia or triplegia

Less common distribution. Be cautious with diagnosis and exclude alternative causes if the pattern is atypical.

Pathophysiology logic

Primary impairments

Motor pathway lesion → spasticity, dystonia, weakness, impaired postural control, impaired selective motor control, sensory/perceptual problems.

Secondary impairments

Growth plus abnormal muscle activity → contracture, torsional deformity, hip displacement, scoliosis, pain, decreased endurance and participation limitation.

Board exam bridge: Spasticity pattern is not only “tight muscle.” It is the visible result of poor descending regulation, poor reciprocal inhibition, poor postural control and poor selective motor control. This is why treatment must combine tone reduction, strengthening, positioning, motor learning, orthoses and prevention of deformity. [G1][S1]

4. Normal motor development and maturation logic

Early motor development is not a simple reflex-to-cortex ladder. The fetus and infant show spontaneous patterned activity from spinal and brainstem networks, modulated by supraspinal systems. Development gradually moves from broad variation with limited adaptation toward more adaptive selection through sensory experience and trial-and-error learning. [M1]

Primary variability

Early varied movement explores available motor options. Afferent feedback helps sculpt the nervous system, but movement is not yet well adapted to the task.

Secondary variability

The infant increasingly selects the best motor strategy for the situation. For gross, fine and speech motor control this becomes clearer from about 3 to 4 months post-term onward; sucking and swallowing mature earlier, shortly before term.

5. How to check brain maturation in CP examination

Brain maturation testing asks: “Which lower-level pattern is still dominating, and what higher-level control is missing?” Use it together with posture, tone, ROM, selective motor control, feeding and hand function. [U2][U3]

Clinical link: brain maturation, spasticity and feeding

Extensor thrust can appear as total body extension in supine, prone, sitting and feeding. During feeding it may cause neck extension, poor jaw stability, poor tongue control and increased aspiration risk.

ATNR persistence interferes with midline hand use. When the head turns, the arm posture changes, making hand-to-mouth, toy manipulation and self-feeding difficult.

Poor righting/protective reaction means the child cannot stabilize the head and trunk. The hands are then used for support instead of exploration, feeding or fine motor play.

6. Physical examination algorithm

Position rule from your notes: if the child can sit, start sitting. If the child cannot sit, start supine. Undress enough to see posture, give toys, and observe before touching. [U2][U3]

A. History checklist

  • Identity, age, sex, caregiver and referral reason.
  • Chief complaint and current medical diagnosis.
  • Recent milestone and developmental history.
  • Prenatal history: pregnancy, infection, maternal conditions.
  • Perinatal history: delivery, APGAR, preterm, low birth weight, NICU.
  • Postnatal history: infection, jaundice/kernicterus, seizure, trauma.
  • Imaging and supportive examinations: MRI brain, hip X-ray, FEES/VFSS, BERA, vision.
  • Associated problems: seizure, vision, hearing, intellectual, communication, oromotor, GIT, respiratory, bladder-bowel, emotional, malnutrition.
  • Functional problems: feeding/swallowing, ADL, defecation, mobility, ambulation, play, learning.
  • Complications: pain, aspiration, recurrent respiratory infection, GERD, constipation, contracture, equinus, scoliosis, hip subluxation.
  • Rehabilitation history: PT, OT, speech therapy, orthosis, mobility device, botulinum toxin, surgery, home program, progress.
  • Environment and personal factors: caregiver capacity, siblings, peer support, finances, education of mother, parenting pattern, behavior, psychological issues.

B. 30-minute exam sequence

1Observe
Sitting or supine with toys. Nutrition, posture, head/trunk, communication, oromotor, breathing, UE/LE movement.
2Classify
GMFCS, MACS, CFCS, EDACS impression. State motor type and topography.
3Hands-on supine
Tone, ROM, clonus/reflexes, pain, hip/knee/ankle tests, brain maturation reflexes.
4Prone and sitting
Ely, hip rotation, thigh-foot angle, spine, postural control, sitting balance.
5Standing/gait
Alignment, balance, orthosis checkout, gait pattern. End with ICF problem list and prescription.

Spastic quadriplegic CP vicious cycle

Weak and inactive postural muscles of neck and trunk can lead to compensation with shoulder elevation, scapular adduction/internal rotation, cervical extension and total body extension. This pattern restricts diaphragmatic mechanics, limits head-midline control, impairs feeding posture and promotes secondary consequences such as dysphagia, GERD, drooling, respiratory disease, constipation, hip subluxation, scoliosis, pain, sleep disorder and sensory processing difficulties. [W1]

8. Spasticity and tone management

Spasticity

Velocity-dependent increase in resistance to passive stretch, often with hyperreflexia and clonus. In CP it coexists with weakness, impaired selective motor control and abnormal postural control.

Dystonia

Intermittent or sustained involuntary muscle contractions causing abnormal posture or twisting movement. It often fluctuates with effort, emotion, pain, position or task.

9. Gait patterns in CP

Clinical gait analysis identifies deviations through video, spatiotemporal data, kinematics, kinetics, EMG and plantar pressure. It does not automatically tell you treatment, but it helps you identify gait deviations, link them with impairments and choose a coherent intervention. [G1]

Gait analysis interpretation algorithm

1Observe
Foot contact, knee, hip, pelvis, trunk, arm swing, symmetry, speed.
2Name pattern
Hemiplegic type or diplegic true equinus, jump, apparent equinus, crouch.
3Find impairment
Spasticity, weakness, contracture, torsion, lever arm dysfunction, poor selective control.
4Separate compensation
Example: apparent equinus may be hip/knee flexion, not fixed ankle equinus.
5Treat by goal
Preserve strength, restore lever arm, limit deformity, improve safety and efficiency.

10. Prognosis

Prognosis in CP is functional. State prognosis for walking, hand function, communication, feeding, participation and complications. Do not give walking prognosis from age alone.

A. Walking prognosis

Good signs

  • Independent sitting before about 2 years is generally favorable.
  • Crawling on hands and knees by about 1.5 to 2.5 years is favorable.
  • Progress toward walking before 4 years is favorable.
  • Hemiplegic and diplegic patterns generally have better ambulation potential than severe quadriplegic patterns.

Poor signs

  • No independent sitting by 4 years suggests poor walking prognosis.
  • Persistent primitive reflexes after 12 months are poor prognostic signs.
  • GMFCS IV-V, poor head control, absent protective reactions and severe hip/trunk involvement worsen prognosis.

11. Rehabilitation management

A. Global management algorithm

1Set ICF diagnosis
Body structure/function, activity, participation, environment.
2Prioritize risk
Feeding safety, aspiration, pain, seizure, hip, respiratory, nutrition.
3Prescribe therapy
PT/OT/ST with goals, dose, home program and caregiver training.
4Add devices
Orthosis, seating, standing frame, mobility aid, AAC.
5Review
Progress, tolerance, skin, sleep, family burden, complications.

B. Practical prescriptions

Postural control and mobility

  • Goal: improve head, trunk and hip control; reduce total body extension or sacral sitting; prepare for sitting, standing, transfers or gait.
  • Intervention: developmental handling, task-specific transitions, sitting balance, prone or forearm support, supported standing, reaching in midline.
  • Dose: PT 2-5 sessions/week depending severity and access, plus caregiver home program daily in short repeated blocks.
  • Home cue: “position before movement.” Align pelvis and trunk before asking for hand or oral function.

Stretching and contracture prevention

  • Targets: hip flexors/adductors, hamstrings, gastrosoleus, rectus femoris, wrist/finger flexors, thumb adductors based on exam.
  • Dose: daily low-load prolonged stretch, combined with positioning, splints or serial casting if needed.
  • Principle: stretch is not enough if spasticity, weakness and postural control are not addressed.

Strengthening and motor control

  • Targets: antigravity trunk extensors/flexors, hip abductors/extensors, knee extensors, ankle dorsiflexors or plantarflexors as indicated.
  • Dose: progressive strengthening for at least 6-8 weeks, 2-3 days/week for structured training, embedded in play and function daily.
  • Safety: monitor fatigue, pain, seizure control, osteoporosis/fracture risk, respiratory compromise and aspiration risk.

Standing program

  • Indication: GMFCS III-V with limited independent standing, prevention of hip/knee/ankle contracture, bone and bowel support, caregiver transfer goals.
  • Dose: build toward around 45-60 minutes/day or accumulated daily standing if tolerated, with good hip-knee-ankle alignment.
  • Device: standing frame, parapodium/back slab, KAFO/HKAFO or supported standing system depending trunk and hip control.

Feeding and swallowing

  • Screen: EDACS, cough/choke, wet voice, recurrent chest infection, feeding duration, weight gain, caregiver stress.
  • Position: upright stable sitting, pelvis supported, head midline, slight chin tuck if appropriate, avoid uncontrolled neck extension.
  • Intervention: texture modification, pacing, spoon placement, lip/jaw/tongue facilitation, oral hygiene, FEES/VFSS if aspiration concern, nutrition consult or tube feeding when unsafe or insufficient intake.

Respiratory and secretion management

  • Assess: breathing pattern, chest expansion, retractions, cough strength, secretion retention, aspiration risk, scoliosis and GERD.
  • Intervention: positioning, chest mobility, airway clearance, assisted cough, suction if needed, caregiver training, coordinate with nutrition/feeding plan.
  • Avoid: aggressive techniques without checking contraindications such as severe reflux, unstable cardiorespiratory status, fracture risk or poor tolerance.

Communication and participation

  • Assess: hearing, vision, attention, receptive/expressive language, phonation, resonance and articulation.
  • Intervention: call-response technique, play-based interaction, AAC for children above 3 years or earlier if needed, school/community participation plan.

Hand function and ADL

  • Assess: MACS, thumb-in-palm, wrist/finger position, active grasp-release, bimanual use, sensation/stereognosis.
  • Intervention: stretching, thumb/wrist splinting, task-oriented practice, bimanual training, CIMT for appropriate hemiparetic children, play-based ADL practice.

Pain and caregiver burden

  • Look for: hip dislocation, constipation, GERD, dystonia/spasm, fracture, skin pressure, dental pain, post-operative pain.
  • Intervention: treat generator, positioning, stretching, analgesia with pediatric team, modalities when appropriate, caregiver handling education.

C. Multidisciplinary team

Core team usually includes PM&R physician, developmental pediatrician or pediatric neurologist, orthopedic surgeon, physiotherapist, occupational therapist, speech-language therapist, orthotist, nutritionist, psychologist, nurse, social worker and educators. The family is part of the treatment team. [N1][S1]

C. AFO and shoe checkout checklist from your notes

  • Is the orthosis consistent with the prescription and treatment goal?
  • Can the child wear and remove it safely with caregiver help?
  • Is the shoe appropriate, comfortable and wide/deep enough?
  • Does the shoe sole provide adequate grip?
  • Is the metatarsophalangeal flexion line positioned correctly?
  • Is shoe length about 1 cm longer than the foot?
  • Does the heel counter adequately control the heel?
  • Is the toe box comfortable and not compressing toes?
  • Is there enough room for the AFO or insert without excessive pressure?
  • Is ankle alignment corrected so foot and shank are well approximated?
  • Does the child have neutral shank alignment and appropriate upright position?
  • Are both uprights placed along the midline of the leg?
  • Are the uprights long enough and not impinging at the knee?
  • Are cuffs and bands at the correct width, height and tightness?
  • Is pressure avoided over fibular head, malleoli, navicular and metatarsal heads?
  • After standing/walking, check skin redness, pain, sleep disturbance and functional improvement.
Removal rule: remove the orthosis and reassess if pain persists despite repositioning, strap adjustment or pressure relief. Review fit at every contact. [S1]

13. Oral exam scripts

One-minute CP case opening

“This child has a non-progressive developmental brain disorder causing abnormal movement and posture. I will classify the CP by motor type, topography and function using GMFCS, MACS, CFCS and EDACS. My examination will start with observation in the most functional position, then assess postural control, communication, oromotor and respiratory function, tone, ROM, primitive reflexes, musculoskeletal deformity, standing and gait or mobility. My rehab plan will target function and participation while preventing aspiration, hip displacement, scoliosis, contracture, pain and caregiver burden.”

How to explain spasticity pattern

“The spastic posture is not only tightness. It reflects impaired descending control and poor selective motor control, so the child uses mass synergy instead of isolated movement. I need to differentiate dynamic spasticity from fixed contracture and bony deformity, because dynamic tone may respond to therapy, orthosis, casting or BoNT-A, while fixed deformity may require orthopedic management.”

How to explain feeding in severe CP

“Feeding is a posture-dependent motor task. Poor head and trunk control, cervical extension, jaw instability, tongue thrust and poor respiratory coordination can cause inefficient and unsafe swallowing. I will assess EDACS level, feeding duration, cough or choking, recurrent chest infection and nutrition, then optimize positioning, texture, pacing, oral motor control and consider FEES/VFSS and nutrition support.”

How to explain gait pattern

“I will not prescribe only from the gait appearance. I will identify the pattern, then link it to impairments such as spasticity, weakness, contracture, torsion and lever arm dysfunction. For example, apparent equinus can be caused by hip and knee flexion despite normal ankle dorsiflexion, so Achilles treatment alone would be wrong.”
