1. First, map the low back pain problem

For PM&R, low back pain is not only a pain diagnosis. It is a functional problem involving tissue irritability, movement control, neurologic safety, psychosocial risk, work demands, and recurrence prevention.

Category 1

Nonspecific mechanical LBP

Most common. Pain arises from muscle, ligament, disc, facet, or movement-system dysfunction, but no single dangerous structural pathology dominates.

Category 2

Radiculopathy or stenosis

Leg pain, paresthesia, weakness, reflex change, neurogenic claudication, or positive neural tension testing suggests neural involvement.

Category 3

Referred nonspinal pain

Renal, vascular, gastrointestinal, pelvic, or systemic conditions can present as back pain. Pattern recognition matters.

Category 4

Specific spinal pathology

Fracture, infection, malignancy, inflammatory disease, cauda equina syndrome, or progressive neurologic compromise.

Board-exam anchor: classify first, then decide whether the patient needs reassurance and active rehabilitation, imaging, laboratory tests, pain intervention, or urgent referral.
The PM&R mental model
Screen safety. Red flags, neurologic deficits, bladder/bowel symptoms, fever, cancer history, trauma, corticosteroid use, osteoporosis, inflammatory signs.
Identify pain generator pattern. Mechanical axial pain, discogenic pain, facet pain, SIJ pain, radicular pain, stenotic claudication, or referred pain.
Assess function. Sitting, standing, walking tolerance, sleep, work, lifting, fear avoidance, self-efficacy, recurrence history.
Choose entry point. Education, graded activity, directional preference, motor control, strengthening, endurance, manual therapy, medication, or referral.
Prevent chronicity. Reduce fear, restore confidence, build capacity, modify exposure, and return the patient to meaningful tasks.

2. Basic anatomy you must own

The lumbar spine is built from repeated motion segments. Each segment contains a disc, paired facet joints, ligaments, muscles, and exiting/traversing nerve roots.

Click a structure

Use the diagram to revise the motion segment. Start with the disc, facet joints, canal, and nerve root.

Key tissue pain generators

Intervertebral disc

Annulus fibrosus and endplate can become nociceptive. Degeneration involves proteoglycan loss, dehydration, annular fissure, abnormal load transfer, and inflammatory mediator release.

Facet joint

Synovial joint. Extension and rotation increase facet loading. Capsular strain, synovitis, cartilage wear, and osteophytes may cause axial or referred pain.

Nerve root

Compression plus chemical inflammation produces radicular pain. Neurologic examination localizes affected root through myotome, dermatome, and reflex change.

3. Lumbar biomechanics: how loading becomes symptoms

The spine works as a load-bearing column and a movement system. Pain often appears when tissue capacity is lower than the mechanical demand.

Flexion

Increases posterior annular tension and may provoke discogenic or radicular symptoms in flexion-sensitive patients. It also opens facets and can relieve stenotic symptoms.

Extension

Increases facet approximation and may narrow the canal/foramina. It may relieve posterior disc derangement in some directional-preference patterns.

Rotation

Coupled with flexion or compression, rotation increases annular and facet stress. Repetitive twisting is relevant in occupational LBP.

Disc-facet partnership: the disc resists compression and distributes load; facets resist shear and guide movement. Disc height loss can shift more load to posterior elements and alter foraminal size.

Passive subsystem

Vertebrae, discs, facets, ligaments. Provides structural limits and joint stiffness.

Active subsystem

Trunk and hip muscles: multifidus, transversus abdominis, erector spinae, quadratus lumborum, gluteals, diaphragm, pelvic floor.

Neural subsystem

Motor control, proprioception, timing, threat perception, and anticipatory stabilization.

Motor control training is logical when the problem is not only weakness but poor timing, guarding, fear-based movement avoidance, or loss of segmental control.

Lumbopelvic rhythm is the coordinated sagittal-plane movement between the lumbar spine, pelvis, and hips during forward bending and return to upright. Clinically, it answers this question: is the patient moving through the hips and pelvis, or are they overusing a painful lumbar segment?

Forward bending

Normal movement uses both lumbar flexion and anterior pelvic rotation over the femoral heads. A practical teaching estimate is that full trunk flexion is roughly shared between lumbar flexion and hip/pelvic motion, with a larger total contribution from the hips/pelvis in end range.

Return from flexion

The hip extensors and lumbar extensors reverse the movement. Poor gluteal control, hamstring guarding, or fear of movement may cause a jerky return, early lumbar extension, or painful lumbar hinging.

Clinical implication

Reduced hip mobility, hamstring tightness, hip flexion contracture, weak gluteals, or poor hip-hinge skill can shift load toward the lumbar spine and perpetuate recurrent low back pain.

How to observe it quickly

ObservationPossible interpretationRehabilitation cue
Excessive lumbar flexion with little pelvic rotationLimited hip flexion, hamstring stiffness, poor hip-hinge strategyTeach hip hinge, improve posterior-chain mobility, strengthen gluteals.

Exam phrase to memorize

“I assess lumbopelvic rhythm because low back pain may result from poor load sharing between the lumbar spine and hips. If hip motion or pelvic control is limited, the lumbar spine may compensate with excessive flexion, extension, or segmental hinging.”

Common maladaptive movement patterns
  • Flexion dominant: repeated flexion with limited hip hinge, often provokes discogenic pattern.
  • Extension dominant: excessive lordosis/hinging at lower lumbar segments, often provokes facet or pars stress.
  • Rotation dominant: trunk twists rather than hip/thoracic contribution, relevant for sport and work.
  • Guarded movement: global stiffness due to pain threat, fear avoidance, or central sensitization.
  • Deconditioned pattern: low endurance and poor load tolerance after prolonged rest.

4. Common pathology and pathophysiology

Learn the pain behavior. The exact MRI finding is often less important than whether the clinical pattern fits the patient.

Mechanical

Muscle strain / ligament sprain

Pathophysiology: overload, eccentric microtearing, inflammatory mediator release, reflex spasm, restricted motion.

Pain behavior: localized pain, worse with movement, tender paraspinals, usually normal neurologic exam.

PM&R key: reassure, relative rest only briefly, progressive mobility and strengthening.

Disc

Discogenic pain

Pathophysiology: annular fissure, endplate injury, proteoglycan loss, disc dehydration, abnormal load transfer, nociceptive and vascular ingrowth.

Pain behavior: axial pain often worse with sitting, flexion, cough/strain if irritated.

PM&R key: directional preference, graded exposure, trunk endurance, avoid excessive fear around imaging.

Neural

Lumbar disc herniation with radiculopathy

Pathophysiology: mechanical compression plus chemical radiculitis.

Pain behavior: leg pain below knee, dermatomal sensory changes, myotomal weakness, reflex loss, positive SLR/crossed SLR.

PM&R key: neuro screen repeatedly, modify irritability, consider MRI/referral if severe or persistent.

Facet

Facet arthropathy

Pathophysiology: synovial joint degeneration, capsular stretch, osteophytes, local inflammation.

Pain behavior: axial LBP, may refer to buttock/thigh, worse extension/rotation, usually not below knee.

PM&R key: extension control, hip mobility, trunk endurance, diagnostic blocks if interventional planning.

SIJ

Sacroiliac joint pain

Pathophysiology: load-transfer dysfunction or inflammatory SI involvement.

Pain behavior: buttock/posterior pelvic pain, Fortin area, provoked by transitions, single-leg loading, several positive SI provocation tests.

PM&R key: pelvic girdle strengthening, load management, image-guided diagnostic injection when needed.

Stenosis

Lumbar spinal stenosis

Pathophysiology: degenerative canal/foraminal narrowing from disc bulge, facet hypertrophy, ligamentum flavum thickening, spondylolisthesis.

Pain behavior: neurogenic claudication, worse standing/walking/extension, better sitting/flexion.

PM&R key: flexion-biased conditioning, walking/cycling tolerance, neurologic monitoring, referral if progressive deficit.

Young athlete

Spondylolysis / spondylolisthesis

Pathophysiology: pars stress injury, often extension/rotation loading.

Pain behavior: extension-provoked LBP, hamstring tightness, positive Stork test sometimes, adolescent athlete profile.

PM&R key: activity modification, neutral-spine strengthening, return-to-sport progression.

Red flag

Compression fracture

Pathophysiology: trauma, osteoporosis, chronic steroid use, malignancy-related weakening.

Pain behavior: focal midline pain, point tenderness, worse flexion/loading, older age or risk factors.

PM&R key: image when suspected, analgesia, bracing selectively, osteoporosis management, safe mobilization.

Systemic

Infection or malignancy

Pathophysiology: vertebral osteomyelitis, discitis, epidural abscess, metastatic disease, hematologic malignancy.

Pain behavior: fever, night pain, unremitting rest pain, weight loss, immunosuppression, IV drug use, cancer history.

PM&R key: do not treat as routine mechanical LBP. Expedite imaging/labs/referral.

Chronic pain

Central sensitization and yellow flags

Pathophysiology: persistent nociceptive drive plus altered pain modulation, threat perception, sleep disturbance, mood, fear avoidance.

Pain behavior: disproportionate pain, widespread symptoms, high disability, fear of movement.

PM&R key: pain neuroscience education, graded exposure, CBT-informed rehab, functional goals.

5. Evaluation: clinical reasoning sequence

History and physical examination are sufficient for most atraumatic acute low back pain when red flags are absent. Imaging should serve the clinical question, not replace the examination.

Emergency pattern: new urinary retention or overflow incontinence, saddle anesthesia, bowel dysfunction, bilateral sciatica, rapidly progressive weakness, fever with neurologic deficit, suspected unstable fracture, or cancer/infection with neurologic compromise.

Do not image immediately when...

Atraumatic acute nonspecific LBP, no red flags, no progressive neurologic deficit, and symptoms fit a mechanical pattern.

Consider imaging when...

Persistent symptoms beyond about 6 weeks despite adequate conservative care, progressive neurologic deficit, suspected infection, malignancy, fracture, inflammatory disease, or cauda equina syndrome.

6. Management options: from first visit to referral

The safest core management is education, staying active as tolerated, and progressive rehabilitation. More invasive options are reserved for selected patterns and failed conservative care.

Acute/subacute

First-line

Reassurance, education, remain active, avoid bed rest, avoid heavy provocative activity briefly, superficial heat, simple analgesic strategy when needed, early function restoration.

Chronic

First-line

Exercise therapy, motor control or trunk endurance training, graded activity/exposure, CBT-informed approach, sleep and lifestyle management, work participation planning.

Radicular/stenotic

Selected care

NSAID if appropriate, activity modification, neural mobility when tolerated, directional preference, flexion-biased conditioning for stenosis, epidural steroid injection or surgical evaluation in selected cases.

Lumbar bracing and orthosis decision-making
Key principle: lumbar bracing is an adjunct for selected indications, not routine long-term treatment for uncomplicated nonspecific LBP.

When I would prescribe bracing

  • Short-term support for severe acute pain when it helps the patient mobilize safely.
  • Stable vertebral compression fracture or post-operative/post-trauma protection according to spine-team restrictions.
  • Symptomatic spondylolisthesis, pars stress injury, or mechanical instability pattern where motion control helps symptoms.
  • SIJ/pelvic girdle pain where pelvic compression improves transfer, gait, or single-leg loading.

When I would avoid routine bracing

  • Uncomplicated nonspecific LBP that can be managed with education, activity, and exercise.
  • Long-term passive use without functional gains.
  • Patient belief that the spine is fragile and cannot move without the brace.

Typical instruction: wear during painful upright tasks or higher-risk transitions for a limited period, remove during rest and supervised exercise unless otherwise indicated, reassess within 1-2 weeks, and start weaning as pain and confidence improve.

Referral logic
  • Urgent surgical referral: cauda equina syndrome, progressive/severe neurologic deficit, suspected spinal infection with neurologic compromise, unstable fracture, malignancy with neurologic compromise.
  • Pain management referral: persistent function-limiting pain despite adequate conservative care, refractory radicular or stenotic symptoms, need for diagnostic or therapeutic injections.
  • Rheumatology: inflammatory back pain pattern, morning stiffness, sacroiliitis, systemic inflammatory features.
  • Psychology/behavioral health: high fear avoidance, catastrophizing, depression/anxiety, trauma history, disproportionate disability.

8. PM&R rehabilitation programming

A good LBP program is chosen by irritability, directional preference, neurologic safety, psychosocial profile, and functional goal.

Phase 1: calm symptoms and protect safety. Screen red flags, reduce irritability, teach relative activity, walking, comfortable positions, heat or analgesia if needed.
Phase 2: restore movement. Directional preference, gentle mobility, hip and thoracic contribution, neural mobility if tolerated.
Phase 3: rebuild control and capacity. Trunk motor control, multifidus/TrA coordination, gluteal strengthening, progressive endurance.
Phase 4: task-specific loading. Lifting, carrying, sitting tolerance, occupational tasks, sport-specific trunk and hip demands.
Phase 5: recurrence prevention. Independent program, graded exposure to feared tasks, lifestyle risk modification, relapse plan.

Acute irritable LBP

Frequency: short bouts daily. Intensity: comfortable, non-threatening movement. Time: 5-10 minutes per bout. Type: walking, breathing, pelvic tilt, gentle directional preference, heat if useful.

Subacute recovery

Frequency: 3-5 exercise days/week. Intensity: low-moderate. Time: 20-30 minutes. Type: mobility, motor control, hip strengthening, trunk endurance, graded ADL exposure.

Chronic/recurrent LBP

Frequency: aerobic most days, strengthening 2-3 days/week, motor control 3-5 days/week. Intensity: progressive but tolerable. Type: multimodal exercise, trunk endurance, aerobic, graded exposure.

Stenosis pattern

Frequency: most days. Intensity: light-moderate. Type: flexion-biased conditioning such as cycling, treadmill with slight incline if tolerated, hip mobility, lower limb strengthening.

How to progress safely
  • Use the “24-hour response”: symptoms during exercise are acceptable if mild/moderate and return to baseline by the next day.
  • Progress only one variable at a time: range, duration, resistance, speed, complexity, or frequency.
  • If pain peripheralizes, neurologic symptoms worsen, or function drops, reduce load and reassess diagnosis.
Return-to-work reasoning
  • Define job demands: sitting, standing, lifting, twisting, vibration, driving, night shift, psychosocial stress.
  • Use graded return: reduced load, shorter duration, task rotation, ergonomic changes, progressive exposure.
  • Functional tests should reflect the job: loaded carry, floor-to-waist lift, sustained sitting tolerance, repeated sit-to-stand.
Return-to-sport reasoning
  • Restore pain-free baseline movement, then trunk endurance and hip control.
  • Progress from linear movement to multidirectional, rotation, speed, and sport-specific load.
  • Return criteria: stable neurologic status, tolerable symptoms, no next-day flare beyond expected, adequate strength/endurance, confidence.

What to say

“Most acute low back pain improves. The spine is strong. We avoid prolonged bed rest because movement helps recovery. The goal is to find safe movement and gradually rebuild capacity.”

What not to say

Avoid language that makes the patient feel fragile, such as “your spine is worn out,” “your disc is crushed,” or “you must never bend.”
