Anatomy and biomechanics

Key structures that explain pain, weakness, instability, and rehabilitation priorities.

Core concept for exam: shoulder motion is not only glenohumeral motion. It is the integrated movement of the sternoclavicular, acromioclavicular, glenohumeral, and scapulothoracic components. Dysfunction in one part can disturb the entire shoulder complex. S1 S2

Six kinematic principles of shoulder abduction

Scapulohumeral rhythm: full abduction near 180 degrees is classically explained by 120 degrees of glenohumeral abduction and 60 degrees of scapulothoracic upward rotation.
Upward scapular rotation: the 60 degrees of scapular upward rotation results from clavicular elevation at the SC joint and upward rotation at the AC joint.
Clavicular retraction: the clavicle retracts at the SC joint during full abduction.
Scapular posterior tilt: near full elevation, the scapula posteriorly tilts and may externally rotate slightly.
Clavicular posterior rotation: the clavicle rotates posteriorly around its long axis, which helps free scapular upward rotation.
Humeral external rotation: the humerus naturally externally rotates during abduction, moving the greater tubercle away from the acromion to reduce impingement risk.
Rotator cuff force couple in one clinical sentence

The deltoid elevates the humerus but tends to create superior humeral head translation. The rotator cuff counters this by compressing the humeral head into the glenoid and producing inferior/depressive and rotational components that maintain subacromial clearance. This is why rotator cuff tear can present as weakness, painful arc, altered scapular mechanics, and functional instability. S2 S10

Pathology and differential diagnosis

From mechanism to clinical pattern.

Macrotrauma

Acute tear after a clear traumatic event, often in younger or active patients. A fall, sudden traction, shoulder dislocation, or heavy load may produce a full-thickness tear. If there is pre-existing degeneration, smaller forces can still produce a complete tear.

Microtrauma and degeneration

Repeated overload, age-related tendon changes, hypovascularity, metabolic risk factors, and insufficient tendon healing can progress into degenerative partial or full-thickness tearing. This pattern is more common with age. S2 S3

Factors that predict poorer tendon healing after repair

Tear factors

Chronic duration, greater retraction, larger tear size, transverse tear pattern, and massive tear morphology.

Muscle factors

Muscle atrophy and fatty infiltration, especially in chronic degenerative tears.

Patient factors

Older age, smoking, hypercholesterolemia, diabetes, high BMI, and reduced tissue quality may affect outcomes. S3 S13

Physical examination sequence

A practical, time-efficient shoulder exam for PM&R oral or bedside examination.

Opening and safety: confirm identity, dominant hand, occupation/sport, trauma history, surgery date if post-op, red flags, pain score, medications, and precautions from the surgeon.
Inspection from front, side, and back: posture, cervical alignment, shoulder height, clavicle/AC deformity, scapular winging or dyskinesis, deltoid/supraspinatus/infraspinatus wasting, scars, swelling, bruising.
Palpation: SC joint, clavicle, AC joint, coracoid, bicipital groove, greater tubercle, subacromial space, scapular borders, trapezius, levator scapulae, posterior cuff.
Screen cervical spine and neurovascular status: cervical ROM, Spurling or distraction if radicular symptoms, dermatomes, myotomes, reflexes, distal pulses, sensation over lateral shoulder for axillary nerve.
Active ROM: flexion, abduction, extension, external rotation, internal rotation behind back, horizontal adduction. Note painful arc, substitution, shrug sign, and scapular rhythm.
Passive ROM: compare to active ROM. A large AROM-PROM gap suggests weakness or pain inhibition. A global PROM loss suggests capsular stiffness or adhesive capsulitis.
Resisted isometric testing: abduction, external rotation, internal rotation, elbow flexion/supination, scapular retraction/protraction. Differentiate pain-limited strength from true power loss.
Special tests by hypothesis: rotator cuff, impingement, biceps/labrum, AC joint, instability, scapular dyskinesis. Use clusters rather than one isolated test.
Function and goals: reach overhead, hand to mouth, hand behind head, hand behind back, lifting, dressing, grooming, work tasks, sport-specific movements.
Summary: diagnosis impression, impairments, activity limitations, precautions, rehab phase, short-term goals, exercise prescription, and referral/imaging decision.

Cluster for rotator cuff tear

In the notes, the combination of active painful arc, positive drop arm, and weakness in external rotation is emphasized as a practical cluster. Three negative findings make a full-thickness tear less likely, while multiple positive findings justify imaging or surgical consultation depending on context. S2 S8

Active versus passive ROM

AROM limitation with near-normal PROM suggests pain inhibition, weakness, or neurologic deficit. Both AROM and PROM limitation suggests capsular stiffness, adhesive capsulitis, severe pain irritability, or joint restriction.

True weakness versus pain inhibition

True weakness persists despite pain control and often shows lag, drop arm, atrophy, or large AROM-PROM gap. Pain inhibition improves after analgesia or position modification.

Scapular dyskinesis

Early shrugging, winging, excessive anterior tilt, or poor upward rotation may be primary motor control dysfunction or secondary compensation from pain, stiffness, or cuff weakness.

Clinical algorithms

Use these to explain your reasoning during the exam.

Shoulder pain diagnostic algorithm

1. History
Trauma, age, overhead work/sport, night pain, weakness, instability, neck symptoms, previous surgery.
→
2. Red flags
Fever, malignancy history, unexplained weight loss, fracture/dislocation signs, infection, neurologic deficit.
3. AROM/PROM pattern
AROM lost more than PROM: cuff weakness or inhibition. PROM globally restricted: capsular stiffness.
→
4. Localize tissue
Cuff, biceps/labrum, AC joint, cervical, instability, scapular motor control.
5. Test cluster
Use multiple tests: painful arc + ER weakness + drop arm or lag signs for cuff disease.
→
6. Decide management
Conservative rehab, imaging, injection discussion, or surgical referral depending on tear size, trauma, weakness, function, and patient goals.

ScenarioWhy it mattersAction

Persistent weakness, positive lag/drop arm, or atrophySuggests structural cuff failure rather than only pain inhibitionUltrasound or MRI depending availability and surgical planning needs
Conservative care fails after adequate trialPersistent pain or functional limitation despite exercise and load managementReview diagnosis, consider imaging, injection, or surgical consultation
Post-operative sudden pain or loss of functionPossible retear, anchor issue, infection, stiffness, or inappropriate load progressionContact surgeon and modify rehabilitation

AAOS 2025 states that clinical examination can diagnose or stratify patients with rotator cuff tears, but combinations of tests improve diagnostic accuracy; MRI, MRA, CT, and ultrasound are useful adjuncts to clinical examination and radiographs. S3

Conservative rehabilitation

For rotator cuff tendinopathy, partial-thickness tears, selected full-thickness tears, and non-operative candidates.

Main exam statement: non-operative management is not passive waiting. It is education, pain modulation, graded loading, scapular and rotator cuff strengthening, mobility restoration, and return-to-activity planning. S4 S13

How to explain conservative care for a small to medium full-thickness tear

AAOS 2025 reports that both physical therapy and operative treatment can significantly improve patient-reported outcomes in symptomatic small to medium full-thickness rotator cuff tears. However, non-operative management may allow tear size, atrophy, and fatty infiltration to progress over 5 to 10 years, so the patient should be followed clinically and structurally when future repair remains an option. S3

Post-operative rotator cuff repair rehabilitation

Phase-based approach after arthroscopic repair. Modify based on surgeon order, tear size, tissue quality, fixation, and subscapularis involvement.

Do not memorize only time: phase progression must consider tissue healing, pain, ROM, neuromuscular control, and repair protection. Large tears, revision repair, poor tissue quality, and subscapularis repair usually require more conservative progression. S5 S6

Tendon healing frame

Inflammation

Immediate response with hematoma, cytokine release, inflammatory cell migration, and removal of debris. Primary rehab concern is protection and pain control.

Proliferation

Cell proliferation and extracellular matrix formation create early scar tissue. Rehab emphasizes protected ROM without excessive tendon load.

Remodeling

Collagen organization shifts toward stronger longitudinal type I collagen alignment. Progressive strengthening becomes more appropriate as tissue matures. S2

Early versus delayed rehabilitation decision

Early motion may be considered when

  • Small tear or single-tendon repair
  • High risk of stiffness such as adhesive capsulitis, calcific tendinitis, PASTA repair, concomitant labral repair, or stiff pre-op shoulder
  • Good tissue quality and stable fixation
  • Patient is reliable and understands restrictions

Delayed or conservative motion is safer when

  • Large or massive tear
  • Revision repair
  • Multiple tendons or subscapularis involvement
  • Poor tissue quality, fatty infiltration, high retear risk
  • Patient cannot reliably avoid active use

Current evidence generally shows similar long-term outcomes between early and delayed mobilization after small to medium arthroscopic rotator cuff repair. Early motion can improve short-term ROM, while delayed mobilization may trend toward higher healing rates, especially when tissue protection is important. S3 S5 S7
