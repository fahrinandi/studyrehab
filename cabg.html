Core exam answer: CABG rehabilitation is not only walking after surgery. It is a risk-stratified, monitored, multidisciplinary program that prevents pulmonary and thromboembolic complications, restores ADL independence, addresses pain and fear of movement, protects the healing sternum, improves functional capacity, and supports secondary prevention.

1. CABG overview

Your notes correctly define CABG as a major surgical revascularization procedure using harvested veins or arteries to bypass obstructed coronary arteries and restore myocardial blood flow, thereby reducing ischemia and symptoms such as angina.[1,2]

What CABG does

CABG creates a new route for blood to pass around an atherosclerotic coronary stenosis. The graft is anastomosed proximally and distally so oxygenated blood can reach myocardium beyond the blocked segment. The clinical goals are relief of angina, improvement of myocardial perfusion, preservation of viable myocardium, and, in selected anatomic groups, improved survival.[1,2]

Common graft materials from your notes

The left internal mammary artery, also called the left internal thoracic artery, is commonly grafted to the left anterior descending artery. Saphenous vein grafts are commonly harvested from the lower limb. Other conduits may include the right internal mammary artery, radial artery, or gastroepiploic artery depending on anatomy and surgical planning.[2]

2. Indications and complications

Your notes list classic 2011 ACCF/AHA Class I CABG indications: left main disease greater than 50%, three-vessel coronary disease greater than 70%, two-vessel disease involving LAD plus another major artery, significant stenosis with persistent angina despite medical therapy, and severe single-vessel disease in a survivor of sudden cardiac death with ischemia-related ventricular tachycardia.[2]

Updated exam nuance: More recent revascularization guidelines emphasize individualized Heart Team decision-making, coronary anatomy complexity, left main disease, diabetes, LV dysfunction, symptoms despite guideline-directed medical therapy, and patient preference. Significant left main disease remains a strong indication where surgical revascularization improves survival compared with medical therapy in appropriate patients.[1]

3. Phases of cardiac rehabilitation after CABG

Your notes focus mainly on the in-ward postoperative period, which is Phase I cardiac rehabilitation. For board examination, place Phase I within the full continuum.

PhaseUsual settingMain goalsCore PM&R actions
Phase IICU, step-down unit, wardPrevent pulmonary and thromboembolic complications, restore safe bed mobility, transfers, walking, ADL basics, and prepare discharge.Risk screen, breathing exercises, coughing, upright sitting, progressive ambulation, education, sternal strategy, discharge plan.
Phase IIEarly outpatient or supervised center-based/home-based programImprove exercise capacity, confidence, risk factor control, and return to meaningful activity.Exercise testing when appropriate, individualized FITT prescription, aerobic plus resistance training, education, psychosocial support.
Phase IIIMaintenance supervised or semi-supervised programProgress endurance and strength, reduce recurrent cardiovascular risk, reinforce long-term habits.Progressive exercise dose, goal review, medication adherence, nutrition, smoking cessation, vocational and recreational planning.

Board-style wording: Phase I begins once the patient is hemodynamically stable after surgery. It is not maximal exercise training. It is supervised functional remobilization with respiratory care, safety monitoring, education, and discharge planning.

4. Phase I inpatient rehabilitation problems from your notes

This section keeps your original problem categories and expands them into exam-ready clinical reasoning.

1. Cardiopulmonary limitations

Decreased lung capacity and atelectasis. General anesthesia, mechanical ventilation, incision pain, diaphragmatic inhibition, pleural irritation, and guarded breathing reduce tidal volume and cough effectiveness. Shallow breathing increases atelectasis and pneumonia risk. The patient may need deep breathing, thoracic expansion, directed cough or huff, splinted coughing, incentive spirometry when prescribed, upright positioning, and progressive walking.

Profound fatigue and low endurance. Sitting at the edge of the bed or first standing may cause dyspnea, tachycardia, orthostatic symptoms, or marked RPE because of surgical stress, anemia, sleep disruption, pain, preoperative deconditioning, and reduced pulmonary volumes.

Arrhythmias and hemodynamic instability. Postoperative atrial fibrillation, orthostatic hypotension, chronotropic abnormality, abnormal BP response, and dizziness can appear during early mobilization. This is why Phase I therapy requires vital sign monitoring before, during, and after activity.

2. Pain and musculoskeletal issues

Sternal precautions and postural guarding. Sternotomy pain often makes patients hunch forward, keep the shoulders adducted, and avoid trunk rotation. This can create neck, shoulder, thoracic, and upper back stiffness. Modern practice increasingly emphasizes controlled movement with the upper arms close to the body rather than arbitrary long-term load restrictions.[4,5]

Incisional discomfort. Pain may come from the chest incision, drain sites, and graft harvest sites. Saphenous vein harvest may make early walking awkward because of leg pain, swelling, or wound sensitivity.

3. Psychological and emotional barriers

Low mobility confidence. Patients often fear that moving, coughing, or walking will damage the heart or tear the incision. This fear can reduce participation and increase immobility-related risk.

Postoperative vulnerability and cognitive haze. ICU lines, monitors, drains, pacing wires, oxygen, sleep deprivation, delirium, and cognitive changes can make patients feel fragile. Depression, anxiety, tearfulness, and poor sleep may reduce motivation. These factors are also recognized in cardiac rehabilitation risk and recovery frameworks.[3,4]

4. Functional deconditioning

Even a short ICU stay causes rapid muscle deconditioning, reduced orthostatic tolerance, gait instability, and difficulty with ADLs. The patient may feel that bed-to-chair transfer, toileting, or walking to the bathroom is a major task. Rehabilitation should restore independence in a staged sequence: rolling, sitting, sit-to-stand, transfer, standing tolerance, marching, corridor walking, stairs if needed, then home activity planning.

Equipment management is a real barrier. Oxygen tubing, IV lines, telemetry, temporary pacing wires, drains, and urinary catheters can make movement mechanically difficult. Before mobilizing, plan the route, secure devices, and coordinate with nursing staff.

5. Edema and fluid retention

Lower-extremity swelling is common after CABG, especially where the saphenous vein was harvested. Edema can make the leg feel tight and heavy, reduce ankle motion, disturb gait, and increase discomfort. Management includes ankle pumps, frequent walking in short bouts, elevation during rest, wound observation, and compression only when medically approved and when arterial status and wound condition are appropriate.

6. Risk stratification

For cardiac rehabilitation, AACVPR risk stratification is commonly used to estimate risk of an event during rehabilitation and to guide monitoring intensity. In the source algorithm, any high-risk criterion classifies the patient as high risk; low risk requires all low-risk features; moderate risk is used when the patient meets neither high nor low-risk standards.[3]

Risk levelCriteriaClinical implication for CABG rehabilitation
High riskLVEF less than 40%, survivor of cardiac arrest or sudden death, complex ventricular dysrhythmia at rest or exercise, MI or cardiac surgery complicated by cardiogenic shock, CHF or post-procedure ischemic signs, abnormal hemodynamics with exercise, significant silent ischemia, symptoms at low exercise levels less than 5 METs, functional capacity less than 5 METs, or clinically significant depression.[3]Closer supervision, slower progression, symptom-limited activity, telemetry when indicated, and careful communication with cardiology and surgical team.
Moderate riskNeither high nor low risk; examples include LVEF 40 to 50%, symptoms at moderate exercise levels, or mild to moderate silent ischemia.[3]Supervised progression with regular HR, BP, rhythm, RPE, SpO₂, and symptom checks.
Low riskLVEF greater than 50%, no complex dysrhythmia, uncomplicated CABG without CHF or post-event ischemia, normal hemodynamic and ECG response, asymptomatic with exercise, functional capacity at least 7 METs, and absence of depression symptoms.[3]Can progress more confidently but still requires sternotomy-aware movement and secondary prevention education.
Practical point: In early inpatient CABG rehabilitation, formal MET capacity may not be available. The algorithm allows functional capacity to be excluded if not measured, but the clinician should compensate with symptom response, vital sign response, operative course, LVEF, rhythm, and mobility tolerance.[3]

7. Algorithms for exam and ward use

Algorithm A. Can I mobilize this patient today?

Step 1. Confirm medical stability.
Check surgeon or cardiology clearance, hemodynamics, rhythm, oxygenation, pain control, bleeding status, fever, mental status, and device safety.
Step 2. Screen red flags.
Do not mobilize or stop planned activity if there is unstable angina, new ischemic ECG change, uncontrolled arrhythmia, symptomatic hypotension, severe dyspnea at rest, SpO₂ persistently below the local target despite oxygen, major bleeding, suspected sternal instability, new neurological deficit, or severe delirium.
Step 3. Define today’s functional target.
Choose the lowest useful goal: sit out of bed, stand, transfer, march in place, walk inside room, corridor ambulation, stairs, or discharge simulation.
Step 4. Prepare the patient and environment.
Teach splinted cough and move-in-the-tube mechanics. Secure lines, oxygen, drains, and telemetry. Pre-plan the route and sitting rest points.
Step 5. Monitor response.
Record HR, BP, SpO₂, RPE, dyspnea, pain, rhythm if available, gait quality, and symptoms before, during, and after activity.
Step 6. Progress, hold, or refer.
Progress if symptoms and vital signs are acceptable. Hold or down-grade if excessive fatigue, dyspnea, dizziness, poor color, chest pain, nausea, abnormal movement quality, or abnormal hemodynamics occur.[4]

Algorithm B. Stop criteria during mobilization

Stop immediately

  • Angina-like chest pressure, jaw tightness, or new ischemic symptoms.
  • Severe dyspnea, accessory muscle overuse, cyanosis, pallor, or poor color.
  • Dizziness, presyncope, syncope, confusion, or new neurological sign.
  • Palpitations with instability or new significant arrhythmia.
  • Sudden wound pain, sternal clicking/instability, or active bleeding.
  • Nausea, diaphoresis, or excessive fatigue out of proportion to activity.

Then do this

  1. Stop activity and sit or lie the patient safely.
  2. Check HR, BP, SpO₂, symptoms, rhythm if available.
  3. Give oxygen or medication only according to local medical order.
  4. Notify the nurse or physician if symptoms persist, vital signs are abnormal, or ischemia/arrhythmia is suspected.
  5. Document activity level, symptoms, vital response, and recovery time.

Algorithm C. Discharge readiness from Phase I

Respiratory safety.
Can perform breathing exercise and effective cough or airway clearance strategy. SpO₂ stable with prescribed oxygen plan.
Functional safety.
Independent or safely assisted bed mobility, transfers, toileting, walking household distance, and stairs if required at home.
Sternal and wound safety.
Understands wound signs, splinted cough, and safe upper-limb mechanics.
Exercise safety.
Understands RPE target, walking dose, warm-up/cool-down, stop symptoms, and whom to contact.
Secondary prevention.
Understands medication adherence, smoking cessation, BP/lipid/diabetes control, nutrition, sleep, stress, and outpatient CR referral.

8. Practical exercise prescription

Prescription must match the phase. In Phase I, the exercise is functional mobilization. In Phase II onward, exercise becomes structured aerobic and resistance training with progressive overload.

Phase I inpatient prescription by postoperative stage

ICU, once stable
Positioning, breathing control, thoracic expansion, supported cough, ankle pumps, active limb ROM, sitting upright, dangling at bedside if allowed. Intensity: very light, RPE 9 to 11 or dyspnea 0 to 2/10. Goal: prevent pulmonary and immobility complications.
POD 1 to 2
Bed mobility using move-in-the-tube technique, sit out of bed, sit-to-stand, transfer to chair, marching in place, short room ambulation. Frequency: several short bouts per day as tolerated. Monitor HR, BP, SpO₂, RPE, symptoms.
POD 2 to 4
Progress corridor walking, repeated sit-to-stand, standing balance, self-care practice, shoulder and thoracic mobility within pain-free range. Use interval walking if fatigue is high, for example 1 to 3 minutes walking followed by seated rest.
Pre-discharge
Increase walking distance, practice stairs if needed, simulate home ADLs, teach independent home walking plan, stop criteria, wound precautions, and outpatient cardiac rehabilitation referral.

9. Respiratory rehabilitation, incentive spirometry, and IMT

Your notes emphasize that CABG patients may have decreased postoperative lung volumes, respiratory muscle weakness, and expiratory flow limitation. These are relevant because postoperative pain, anesthesia, ventilation, and immobility reduce inspiratory depth and cough effectiveness.

Flow-oriented incentive spirometer from your notes

Triflow principle

A typical flow-oriented incentive spirometer, such as a Triflow device, has three chambers in series, each containing a ball. During inspiration, the patient generates sub-atmospheric pressure above the ball, and the ball rises according to inspiratory flow.

In your notes, the first chamber rises at approximately 600 mL/s, the second at 900 mL/s, and the third at 1200 mL/s.

How to teach it

  1. Sit upright with shoulders relaxed.
  2. Seal lips around the mouthpiece.
  3. Inhale slowly and deeply. Avoid fast, forceful, shallow sucking.
  4. Hold inspiration briefly if tolerated.
  5. Exhale normally, rest, and repeat as prescribed.
Important distinction: Incentive spirometry is feedback for lung expansion. IMT is strengthening of inspiratory muscles against resistance. They are not the same intervention.

IMT indications from your notes, expanded

  • Respiratory muscle weakness: low MIP, difficult weaning, shallow breathing, poor inspiratory endurance.
  • Decreased postoperative lung volume: atelectasis risk, low inspiratory capacity, reduced chest expansion from pain or guarding.
  • Limitation of expiratory flow or secretion clearance: indirect benefit through improved inspiratory depth, cough preparation, and ventilation distribution.

10. Sternotomy strategy: from strict precautions to “move in the tube”

Traditional sternal precautions often restrict pushing, pulling, lifting, reaching, and weight-bearing through the arms. Your notes correctly identify that these restrictions can produce fear, postural guarding, and neck or shoulder stiffness. Modern approaches increasingly focus on controlled movement rather than blanket restriction.

Traditional precautions

  • Avoid forceful pushing or pulling.
  • Avoid heavy lifting.
  • Avoid unilateral loaded reaching far from the body.
  • Use a pillow or splinting for cough.
  • Log-roll or move carefully during bed mobility.

These are still used in some hospitals and should follow local surgical protocol.

Move in the tube

  • Keep the upper arms close to the trunk during loaded movement.
  • Use symmetrical, pain-free movements when possible.
  • Avoid long lever arms away from the chest.
  • Resume ADLs within pain-free limits and wound safety.
  • Teach confidence rather than fear of movement.

This approach is supported by clinical literature and is cited in ACPICR 2023 heart surgery considerations.[4,5,6]

11. Edema and graft-site management

Lower limb edema after CABG is common, particularly on the side of saphenous vein harvest. Edema can limit ankle mobility, alter gait, and increase pain. It must be distinguished from infection, DVT, heart failure, and renal-related fluid overload.

13. Viva questions and concise answers

What is the main goal of Phase I cardiac rehabilitation after CABG?
To safely remobilize the patient, prevent pulmonary and thromboembolic complications, restore basic ADL independence, educate on sternotomy-safe movement and home activity, and prepare referral to outpatient cardiac rehabilitation.
Why does atelectasis occur after CABG?
Because general anesthesia, mechanical ventilation, postoperative pain, diaphragmatic inhibition, pleural irritation, supine positioning, and reduced mobility reduce tidal volume and cough effectiveness. This promotes basal lung collapse and secretion retention.
How do you prescribe walking in Phase I?
Use short, frequent, symptom-limited bouts. Start with bed-to-chair, standing, room ambulation, then corridor walking. Monitor HR, BP, SpO₂, RPE, dyspnea, pain, rhythm, dizziness, and wound symptoms. Progress duration and distance before intensity.
What is the difference between incentive spirometry and IMT?
Incentive spirometry provides visual feedback for deep inspiration and lung expansion. IMT strengthens inspiratory muscles by breathing against a threshold or resistive load, ideally prescribed using measured MIP.
What is “move in the tube”?
It is a sternotomy movement strategy that keeps the upper arms close to the body during loaded movement to reduce excessive sternal stress while allowing functional ADLs within pain-free limits.
What makes a post-CABG patient high risk in cardiac rehabilitation?
Examples include LVEF less than 40%, complex ventricular arrhythmias, cardiac arrest survivor, cardiogenic shock or CHF, post-procedure ischemia, abnormal hemodynamics, symptoms at low exercise level, functional capacity less than 5 METs, or clinically significant depression.
How do beta-blockers affect exercise prescription?
They reduce heart rate response, so HR alone may underestimate effort. Combine HR with RPE, dyspnea score, BP response, rhythm, symptoms, and functional tolerance.
When should mobilization be stopped?
Stop for angina-like symptoms, severe dyspnea, dizziness, presyncope, abnormal pallor or cyanosis, significant arrhythmia, symptomatic hypotension, new neurological sign, sternal instability, active bleeding, severe fatigue, nausea, or concerning vital sign response.
What should be included in discharge education?
Walking plan, RPE target, warm-up and cool-down, stop symptoms, wound care signs, sternal movement strategy, coughing technique, medication adherence, risk factor control, nutrition, smoking cessation, sleep, psychosocial support, and outpatient cardiac rehabilitation referral.
